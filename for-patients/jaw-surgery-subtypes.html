The Foundation

What is Orthognathic Surgery?

Orthognathic surgery is utilized principally for skeletodental syndromes that are beyond the control of conventional orthodontic procedures[cite: 9]. While traditional orthodontics focuses mainly on tooth movement, surgical intervention adds another dimension by repositioning the facial bones while it moves the teeth[cite: 20].

The overarching objective is to maximize the aesthetic and functional result[cite: 192]. While correcting the occlusal (bite) relationships is a major clinical endpoint [cite: 60], achieving a favorable soft tissue change is often the primary goal of the treatment plan[cite: 231].

Clinical Indication

Class III: Prognathism & Midface Deficiency

A Class III skeletodental malocclusion can represent a prognathic (prominent) mandible, a retrognathic (deficient) maxilla, or a combination of both[cite: 1088, 1089, 1090, 1091].

The most commonly cited condition is mandibular prognathism, which is characterized by a Class III dental pattern and a concave facial profile[cite: 10]. In cases where the midface is deficient, a maxillary advancement is performed to pull the upper jaw forward, restoring facial balance and correcting the illusion of an overgrown lower jaw.

Clinical Indication

Class II: Retrognathic & Recessed Jaws

Class II malocclusions are frequently related to mandibular retrognathism (a recessed lower jaw)[cite: 599]. This skeletal discrepancy generally presents in two ways:

First, it can result from an actual short or "underdeveloped" mandible[cite: 600]. Second, it can occur when a relatively normal mandible is retrognathically positioned because an overdeveloped maxillary region causes the lower jaw to rotate downward and backward[cite: 601, 602]. Surgery is used to advance the mandible or impact the maxilla, establishing a strong, harmonious profile.

Clinical Indication

Vertical Maxillary Excess & Deep Bite

Vertical maxillary excess occurs when the upper jaw overdevelops vertically. This often results in a "long face" syndrome and is frequently accompanied by an anterior open bite[cite: 622, 623]. Conversely, patients may present with a deep bite "short face" syndrome, where the mandibular dento-alveolar region appears abnormally short, resulting in a disproportionately small lower face height[cite: 607, 608].

Comprehensive Care

Facial Asymmetry, Genioplasty & Sleep Apnea

Orthognathic procedures go beyond simple bite correction. For patients with chin insufficiency, a sliding genioplasty can be utilized to advance the chin (pogonion) independently of the jaw to dramatically improve the profile[cite: 859].

Additionally, jaw surgery is highly effective at correcting severe jaw asymmetry and facial imbalances. For patients suffering from obstructive sleep apnea, advancing the jaws surgically can physically expand the airway, providing significant, long-term health benefits.
